Hierarchial challenges in Azure

Most organizations that have been using Azure follow some variation of the same hierarchical structure. From the top down, the hierarchy begins with Management Groups, which handle governance, policy, and compliance. Below them are Subscriptions, the natural boundaries for resource management, billing, and access control. At the bottom are Resource Groups, which organize resources according to application lifecycle or deployment boundaries.

While each of these constructs has its own purpose, the hierarchy itself is singular and rigid. Resource Groups must live inside Subscriptions, Subscriptions must live inside Management Groups, and Management Groups can only containSubscriptions or other Management Groups.

Every organization using Azure must operate within this rigid structure while still trying to meet its own organizational needs. To achieve this teams do their best to add context for cost management, resiliency, monitoring, and persona-specific requirements. Tools such as Azure Resource Graph, Resource Tags, and Azure Policy help deliver specific insights, but none of them provide a flexible way to organize and manage resources across the hierarchy.

This gap is exactly why Azure Service Groups were introduced.

Azure Service Groups are currently in preview
At the time of writing, Azure Service Groups are in public preview. Treat them accordingly and expect the feature to evolve before it reaches general availability.

What are Azure Service Groups?

Azure Service Groups are Tenant level logical containers aimed at creating flexible hierarchy to groups resources, resource groups and subscriptions across a tenant and across all current hierarchial boundaries. Service Groups exists independent an parallel to the existing hierarchy. As highlighted in the image below.

Tenant Level
Unlike existing hierarchy constructs, Service Groups do not support RBAC or policy inheritance. Permissions assigned at one level of the Service Group hierarchy do not cascade to any groups below it. This makes Service Groups inherently least-privilege and well-suited for persona-based access models.

Key features

Service Groups are built around three core capabilities: hierarchy, flexibility, and aggregation. Together, these enable a level of organizational modeling that Azure’s traditional structure cannot provide.

  • Hierarchy
    • Supports branching structures and parallel grouping models.
    • Allows self-nesting of Service Groups, up to 10 levels deep.
  • Flexibility
    • Members can come from any subscription within the tenant.
    • Supports grouping of resources, resource groups, and subscriptions.
    • A single resource can belong to multiple Service Groups simultaneously.
  • Aggregation
    • Consolidates data, insights, and telemetry from all included members‚Äîregardless of subscription boundaries.
    • Enables visualizing workload- or domain-specific views from the perspective you choose.

Currently available features for Service Groups.

  • REST API for Microsoft.Management and Microsoft.Relationship
  • ARM, Bicep and Terraform support
  • Resource Graph support

Planned

Planned features for Service Groups.

Limitations

  • A maximum of 2000 service group members per subscription
  • During preview the limit is 10000 Service Groups per single tenant
  • Service Groups require a globally unique ID. Two Microsoft Entra tenants can’t have a Service Group with identical IDs.
  • Needs relationship write permissions on the resource in question to add it to the concerning Service Group

Role Based Access Controls

RBAC for Service Groups is straightforward with 3 built-in roles definitions.

  • Service Group Administrator: Manages all aspects of Service Groups and Relationships and is the default role given to users when they create a Service Group. -
  • Service Group Contributor: Manages the creation or the lifecycle of a Service Group. This role allows for all actions except for Role Assignment capabilities.
  • Service Group Reader: Read-only access to service group information and can be assigned to other resources in order to view the connected relationships.

In order to edit the service group or create children service groups, a user must have ‘Service Group Contributor’ at that service group.

Permissions to add resource members
To add members, users must have ‘Service Group Contributor’ on the Service Group and Microsoft.Relationship/write on the resource. When a resource is added to a Service Group essentially a relationship is added on the resource in question, hence the Microsoft.Relationship/write requirement.

Example Scenarios

Because Service Groups are highly flexible, there are countless ways to structure them. The technology is still in its early days, so it’s too soon to define strict best practices. Instead, the goal of the following examples is to offer inspiration and highlight potential organizational patterns you can experiment with as you explore how Service Groups fit into your environment.

Department View

This is often the first view that comes to mind, although not necessarily the first one you should implement. It mirrors your organizational department structure and, where needed, includes the core services each department provides to the business.

Workload View

This view organizes resources according to the workloads or applications running in your Azure environment, bringing both dedicated and shared components together into a single, unified perspective. It is particularly valuable for Product Owners who need end-to-end visibility of their services.

Business Critically View

This view groups resources based on their importance to business operations, ranging from mission critical systems to supporting or low-impact services. It enables teams to quickly assess operational risk, prioritize recovery efforts, and align monitoring and governance with the level of criticality each service represents. When starting out with Service Groups this is often the most valuable view to implement first.

Vendor View

This view groups all resources associated with external vendors or third-party integrations. It provides a clear picture of each vendor’s footprint and impact within your environment, making it easier to manage dependencies, assess security considerations, and maintain operational oversight. You can even grant vendors access to their specific Service Group to increase transparency where appropriate.

License Model View

This view groups resources based on the licensing model of the software or components they rely on. For example, you might separate open-source solutions from commercial or proprietary ones. Within the open-source category, you can further break down resources by license type such as MIT, Apache 2.0, or GPL‚Äîto support compliance checks and policy requirements.

On the commercial side, you can structure Service Groups by vendor or licensing model. For example per-core licensed software, subscription-based services, or enterprise agreements to simplify audit preparation and license compliance.

Service Groups Fundamentals

Before adopting Service Groups at scale, it’s important to establish a clear foundation. Service Groups introduce a flexible logical layer on top of Azure’s rigid hierarchy, but without structure and automation they can quickly become inconsistent or misaligned with business goals. The following principles help ensure that Service Groups deliver real value from day one.

Basics for getting started with Service Groups

  • Prioritize business criticality. Identify what your business truly depends on from day-to-day and use Service Groups to structure visibility, ownership, and operational actions around those critical elements.
  • Standardize your approach early. Have the platform team design the initial top-level Service Group hierarchy‚Äîlayout, naming, structure, and topologies. Then hand ownership of deeper layers to enabling teams and domain experts to model according to business needs.
  • Shift left through automation. Ensure Service Group membership is applied at deployment time. Treat it as part of your resource provisioning process not an afterthought.
  • Use Azure Policy for scale. Implement automated Service Group membership through policy assignments to maintain consistency, reduce human error, and ensure new resources always land in the correct logical groups.
  • Control consumption and sprawl. Like many Azure features, once enabled everyone wants to use it. Set clear guidelines for when and how Service Groups should be created to ensure they improve clarity and service quality rather than creating noise.

Conclusion

Service Groups are the first step in creating a multiverse for resource organization, offering a flexible and logical grouping mechanism outside Azure’s rigid hierarchy. It’s still early days for the feature, but the potential is significant.
